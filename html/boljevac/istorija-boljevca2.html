History of Boljevac

The municipality of Boljevac is located in eastern Serbia and is part of the Zajecar district. It occupies the valley of the river Crni Timok, and is surrounded by several mountains, where the biggest attraction is the mountain Rtanj, which is located at an altitude of 1565 meters. The entire municipality covers 828 square meters, and borders the municipalities of Bor, Zajecar, Sokobanja, Despotovac, Paracin, Knjazevac and Rozanj. According to the 2011 census report, there were 12.994 inhabitants.

During the Turkish rule, in 1455, a census was conducted on the territory of the Vidin Sandzak, which included the current municipality of Boljevac. According to that census, the current villages such as Ilino, Bogovina, Lukovo, Vrbovac, Dobrujevac are also mentioned ... In that census, the churches and monasteries of Lapusnja, Krepicevac and Lozica are also mentioned.

After the creation of the autonomous province of Serbia, which was still part of the Turkish Empire, in 1833 it was part of the province. and today's Municipality of Boljevac. Thanks to that, in 1834, the town of Boljevac was created next to the river Arnauta. Houses and cafes began to be built, and "newcomers" from neighboring towns and surrounding areas began to settle. towns where they begin to open their craft shops.

In 1836, the first village shop was opened, and in 1842, the first village school was opened. Boljevac as a municipality was formed in 1844. By the decree of Prince Miloš Obrenović from 1860, Boljevac became the seat of the Zaječar district, and thanks to that, the number of inhabitants doubled in the next ten years. ž After the relocation of the parish headquarters from Ilin to Boljevac in 1854, the church of St. Elijah was built in 1861 with a donation from the locals of the surrounding villages. On September 10, 1875, Boljevac was declared a town.

At the beginning of the 20th century, with the discovery of coal ore in the Boljevac municipality, a brown coal mine was opened, which was owned by of the Minch Jewish family. Thanks to all that, in 1912, the railway Zajecar-Boljevac-Paracin was built, in order to speed up the export of brown coal ore from these parts.

The Minch family owned the Rtanj mine for a full 40 years, until the end of the Second World War, when it was nationalized, where it was closed due to frequent methane explosions and miners' casualties. This family was generous and humane towards their workers, they built comfortable apartments for miners, organized a private school, built an ambulance, a grocery store. When moving into the apartment, furniture was distributed to each miner free of charge. The owner of the mine, Julius Minch, committed suicide under unexplained circumstances, and since they had no children, the ownership of the mine was taken over by his wife Greta, where she built a chapel on the top of Rtanj in memory of her son-in-law. The chapel was unfortunately demolished with dynamite in 1992, due to the belief that it contained a hidden treasure full of diamonds and gold.
