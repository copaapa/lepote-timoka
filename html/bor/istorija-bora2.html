History of Bor

Bor is the seat of the city of Bor and the Bor district in eastern Serbia. According to the 2011 census, the city had a population of 34,160. This is a multi-ethnic city with a majority Serb population because this is a mining and industrial city engaged in exploitation. and by processing copper and gold ore and other non-ferrous metals.

During the Roman Empire, Bor, more precisely this area was part of the province of Upper Moesia, whose capital was Viminacium. Until the 6th century, this part of Serbia was part of the Eastern Roman Empire, later falling under the control of the Bulgarian Empire, where he later rejoined Byzantium. Finally, in the 15th century, it became part of the Ottoman Empire.

Until the discovery of copper ore in 1903, this town was an ordinary village with a majority Vlach population. Thanks to Franjo Šistek and financier George Weifert, copper ore was discovered and thus the city developed rapidly.

Until the Second World War, the French had the right to exploit the ore, where under their influence, the city developed, by building French quarters, an Orthodox and Catholic church, a hospital, and a new school. After the war, the mine was nationalized.

Bor received the status of the city of Bor in 1947, when it had 11,000 inhabitants. Residents from all parts of the former Yugoslavia came to work in the Bor mining and smelting basin, and the number of other nations and national minorities increased, but it still remained with a majority Serb population.
Today, the mine is owned by the Chinese company Zijin, where the mine still remains the lifeblood of this city.
