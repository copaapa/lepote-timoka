Felix Romuliana

Felix Romuliana (lat. Felix Romuliana) or in Serbian Gamzigrad, is an archaeological site located in Eastern Serbia near Zajecar. It is the residence of the Roman emperor Gaius Valerius Maximianus Galerius (293-311), which has been placed on the UNESCO World Heritage List since June 29, 2007.

The first description of Gamzigrad was given by a German travel writer, Baron Herder, in his travelogue from 1835, entitled "Mining Route in Serbia". Later, in the second half of the 19th century, the Austrian archaeologist Felix Kanitz and many of our archaeologists and historians became interested in Felix Romuliana, but the interest in Gamzigrad died down in the first half of the 20th century. It was only after the end of the Second World War, more precisely in the 1950s, that interest in Gamzigrad revived.

Archaeological excavations began in 1953, but only after thirty years, more precisely in 1984, was the exact origin of this Roman fortification in Eastern Serbia determined. Then the inscription "Felix Romuliana" ("Happy Romuliana") was discovered, where it was determined that it was the palace of the Roman emperor Gaius Valerius, where he dedicated the name of the palace to his mother Romuliana, and the construction was financed by his father-in-law, the then emperor Diocletian. The palace was built between 297-305.

The palace spread over 6.5 hectares, consisting of two fortresses, various palaces, warehouses, camps for soldiers, temples and early Christian places of worship. After the death of Galerius in 311, and due to the frequent attacks of the Huns in the 5th century and later the Avars in the 6th century, the palace was completely abandoned at the end of the 6th century AD.
