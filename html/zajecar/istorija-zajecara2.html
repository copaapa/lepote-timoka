History of Zajecar

Zajecar is a town in the Zajecar district. According to the last census from 2011, the city had a population of 38,165. Zajecar High School was founded in 1836, and immediately after Kragujevac, it is one of the oldest high schools in Serbia.

There are several legends about the origin of this city. This city is first mentioned in the Turkish census from 1446, where it is belonged to the village of Vidin Sandzak and where there were eight families. According to one legend, there were many rabbits on today's territory of Zajecar, so this town was named after them. Namely, it is believed that the name originated from the word "rabbit" ("rabbit" in the Timok dialect), and since it belonged to the Vidin Sandzak, with a predominantly Bulgarian population in the neighborhood, the word "zajecar" in Bulgarian means keeper of rabbits.

The oldest known inhabitants of this area were the Tribals, later the Mezi are mentioned, and then the Timahi, where they are mentioned in ancient Roman and Byzantine writings. Later, with the arrival of the Slavs in the Balkans and the arrival in these parts, the Slavs called themselves the people of Timoče, where around 800 AD they consider themselves a formed Slavic group.

Until the end of the 18th century, these areas were poorly mentioned, more precisely until the outbreak of the First Serbian Uprising in 1804. When the conditions for the uprising in these parts were met, more precisely in 1806, under the leadership of the hajduk Veljko Petrović, prince Milisav and priest Radosav, a revolt arose in these parts and was liberated from the Turks. In 1833, with the Second Hatisherif, both Zajecar and its surroundings fell under Serbian autonomy.
